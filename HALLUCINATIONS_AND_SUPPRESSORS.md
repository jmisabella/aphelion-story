**APHELION — HALLUCINATIONS & SUPPRESSORS DETAIL**

# The Hallucinations

## Nature

Personalized rather than shared - each infected person experiences something different. The virus was engineered by the Architects and released at a predetermined point in the journey as the final phase of the experiment. It is not purely observational. It is behavioral. The hallucinations are designed to psychologically orient infected individuals toward the destination - herding them, pulling at them below the level of conscious understanding. The population is being moved, psychologically if not physically, toward the Architects' intended endpoint.

## Content Variations

- Different infected individuals may experience different manifestations, including but not limited to:

- Geometric patterns or architectural forms that don't match anything aboard the ship - structures that feel ancient or purposeful, as though the mind is constructing something it has no reference for, possibly a subconscious bleed-through of the destination itself.

- Light behaving wrongly - bending, pooling, emanating from surfaces that shouldn't produce it.

- Sounds with no source - voices just below comprehension, tones that feel like language without being language.

- A sense of vast open space in enclosed areas, walls dissolving into something infinite and cold - the void pressing in.

- Shadows moving independently or ahead of the person casting them.

- People or beings who aren't there.

## The Behavioral Component

The one common thread across all infected individuals despite their different visions: a behavioral pull. Infected people find themselves drawn toward certain parts of the ship without knowing why. They wake having walked somewhere in their sleep. They keep returning to the same corridor. They are being called somewhere without understanding the call. This is the Architects' design - not just observation but compulsion.

## Progression

The hallucinations are not immediately fatal but are progressively debilitating. They worsen over time, eroding the ability to distinguish reality, to function, to care for oneself. A person lost deeply enough in hallucination eventually drifts - stops eating, stops maintaining themselves, dissolves slowly. This is a quiet and terrible deterioration rather than sudden death, in keeping with the story's tone of accumulated dread over spectacle.

## Calla's Immunity

Calla does not have the virus - not yet. Her resistance is not genetic or accidental. Lisel, her mother, sensing something deeply wrong long ago without ever having language for it, did something to protect her daughter without fully understanding why. Whether this was a quiet medical intervention, something introduced into Calla's routine care as a child, or something else is left deliberately ambiguous. Lisel may not even fully remember it as a deliberate act.

This immunity is temporary - a ticking clock. Calla will eventually succumb. Her clarity is a window, not a permanent state, which gives her investigation genuine urgency. She is racing against her own biology as much as against the Continuance.

There is a deeper and more unsettling question the story leaves open: did Lisel protect Calla from the virus, or did she unknowingly protect her from something the Architects specifically needed her to experience? Is Calla's clarity a gift - or does it make her an anomaly the experiment never accounted for? This ambiguity feeds directly into the story's quietly hopeful ending. The one thing the Architects could not design for was a mother's unexamined instinct to protect her child.

## Lisel's Infection

Lisel becomes infected at a mid-to-late point in the story. By then Calla is already deep in her investigation. Watching her mother's reality dissolve while knowing the hallucinations were deliberately engineered creates unbearable dramatic and emotional pressure. Lisel survives to the end of the story, though her condition continues to deteriorate.

# The Suppressors: The Continuance

# Nature and Purpose

The Continuance is a small institution that has existed aboard the Aphelion long enough to feel like infrastructure rather than conspiracy. Its stated purpose, passed down through generations, is to maintain order, morale, and the smooth functioning of ship's society. It controls information flow, manages official records, and oversees the archive system within which Calla works. Most members follow protocols handed down by the Architects without fully understanding their origin or purpose. They genuinely believe they are protecting the population. They are not evil. They are institutional. Which is arguably more frightening.

## What Sets the Story in Motion

A signal was received - not by the general population but intercepted immediately and classified by the Continuance. The act of classifying it created a record in the archive system: a ghost file, a gap where something should be. Calla notices the gap because she knows the archive better than anyone alive. Her entry point is not the signal itself but the shape of its absence. An archivist would notice a hole in the record before anyone else would. This triggering event makes the Continuance's suppression suddenly active and urgent rather than dormant institutional habit. They are managing a live situation. Tension escalates on both sides simultaneously.

## The Mentor: Cassian

Male, approximately 51. A senior archivist or Continuance-adjacent figure who guided Calla into her role and recommended her for it years ago. Someone she respects and trusts deeply. He is a mid-level Continuance member who genuinely believes in the institution's protective purpose and does not know the full truth himself. When Calla gets close to something dangerous he warns her off - gently at first, with the warmth of a trusted colleague urging caution. That warmth curdles into something more ominous as Calla pushes further and he is required to act in ways that conflict with who she believed him to be. He is the Continuance's most human and sympathetic face - not evil, trapped inside the same inherited lie as everyone else, just more deeply embedded in its maintenance.

## The Senior Member: Vesper

A separate figure from the mentor - someone who knows significantly more than most Continuance members and has carried that knowledge for a long time. This person has made a private peace with it, or tried to. When Calla finally reaches this person the reader feels the full weight of what it costs to know the truth and choose silence anyway. Not sympathetic exactly, but tragically comprehensible. They believe the population is better off not knowing. They may even be correct that knowing changes nothing practically. That ambiguity is intentional and important.

## What Calla Risks

In the enclosed world of the Aphelion, traditional consequences feel too blunt. What Calla risks is specific to this world. Loss of her archival access and role - which means loss of identity and purpose, since she was born into it. Social quarantine - the Continuance has enough institutional reach to make her untrusted and unreliable within the ship's society. Most chillingly: if she begins telling people what she has found, the Continuance can simply suggest she is already hallucinating. Her clarity is her greatest asset and the Continuance can weaponize doubt about it against her. She can be dismissed, discredited, and isolated without the Continuance ever having to confirm or deny what she knows.
